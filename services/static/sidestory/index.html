Side Story

This Is a Dream. The Nightmare Kind.

How a Knowledge Engineer who got lost in Excel spreadsheets accidentally built something he'd been meaning to build for thirty years.

This is a dream.

Not the inspirational kind. Not the kind you put on a slide deck with a sans-serif font and a picture of a mountain. The other kind — the one where you're running but not moving, where the problem you thought you solved keeps coming back in a slightly different shape, wearing slightly different clothes, sitting in a slightly different conference room.

· · ·

I've been a computer engineer for longer than most current frameworks have existed. My official title is "system architect." What that means in practice is that I spend my days reading Java class hierarchies that nobody fully understands anymore — including the people who wrote them — and Excel specification documents that have outlived every person who originally understood them. I arrange prefabricated components according to other people's specifications and try to make the result look like it was designed rather than assembled.

It's fine work. Someone has to do it.

I sigh a lot.

· · ·

Back in the day, I had a different title: Knowledge Engineer.

It sounds made-up now. It felt real then.

Corporations — backed by national budgets — were pouring serious money into AI. The existing landscape was DOS-based Multiplan, word processors, COBOL for office efficiency, FORTRAN for numerical calculation. Competent. Cold. It felt like filing cabinets all the way down, and I couldn't make myself care about it.

Then the AI boom arrived, and I was in my twenties, and I did what people in their twenties do: I threw myself at it completely.

I took on only AI-related work. I read every paper I could find — there weren't many. I argued with colleagues in small rooms about semantic networks and knowledge representation. The idea was straightforward and, at the time, felt genuinely revolutionary: computers could learn to reason the way humans reasoned. Meaning could be given structure. Structure could think.

Alan Kay's vision of the desktop as a thinking medium. Steve Jobs releasing the Macintosh. Everything pointed toward a coming shift. I believed I was, in some minor way, part of building it.

My title was Knowledge Engineer. It was, genuinely, a dream job.

· · ·

Nothing happened.

The hardware was simply too weak. The math was right. The machines weren't ready. Projects were scaled back quietly, then personnel cuts began, then most initiatives were closed before reaching any practical stage.

The profession of Knowledge Engineer disappeared the way certain job titles do — not with an announcement, but with a gradual absence from job postings, until one day you realize nobody has used the word in years. The people who held that title scattered. Some went into databases. Some went into finance. Some went into management.

I went into Java.

Then Mac applications. Then whatever the current thing was. The title "system architect" arrived eventually, which sounds significant and means, in my case, that I am an architect who spends his time on structural load calculations and the careful arrangement of prefabricated window panel units.

The ideas from my twenties went into a mental drawer. Not discarded. Not forgotten. Just filed under someday, maybe, if the conditions ever change.

The drawer stayed closed for thirty years.

· · ·

Then came the new AI boom, built around what everyone started calling LLMs.

Because of my background, I started receiving requests for this kind of work. I arrived at the first project with something I hadn't felt in a long time: genuine curiosity.

What I found was Excel.

Massive amounts of requirements and data, scattered across spreadsheets. My job was to organize it using conventional manual processes, hand it to the LLM, receive the output, and transcribe the results back into Excel or relational databases. The LLM was impressive. The workflow around it was indistinguishable from what we were doing in 1994, except the forms had more columns and the meetings had better coffee.

It was a nightmare. The specific, fluorescent-lit, nobody-will-believe-this kind.

The failure mode was different from the 1990s — the hardware was no longer the problem. The problem was that nobody had bothered to define what anything meant before asking the machine to organize it. The LLM generated outputs that looked correct and couldn't be verified. The knowledge graphs were beautiful in presentations and unusable in practice. The Excel files grew. The project, eighteen months later, was quietly discontinued.

I had seen this before. Not because I'm perceptive. Because I had been in the room the first time, thirty years earlier, when a different set of people failed for a different set of reasons that felt, from the inside, identical.

They had skipped the hard part.

The hard part is not writing code. The hard part is deciding what things mean — precisely, rigorously, before you touch a keyboard. That discipline doesn't come from the AI. It comes from the human sitting across from it.

· · ·

I am not going to start a venture. I am past that particular dream.

But somewhere in those afternoons — reading Java class files that haven't been meaningfully touched since 2011, watching knowledge graphs get built and abandoned for the same reasons they were abandoned thirty years ago — I started to feel something I can only describe as an old debt coming due.

The drawer. The semantic network. The system that didn't just store information but understood it.

Not a vision. An argument I never finished.

So I pulled the drawer open.

· · ·

And then something slightly alarming happened.

Thirty years of sediment came out all at once.

The architecture formed in my head within about a week. Not because I am unusually clever, but because the conceptual structure had apparently been sitting there unfinished for three decades, waiting for the surrounding conditions to become survivable.

The strangest part, honestly, is the timeline.

People assume a system like this must have taken many years to build.

The ideas did.

The implementation did not.

I began writing Akasha in the middle of April this year.

Apparently thirty years of postponed ontology compress surprisingly well once released.

· · ·

There was one practical problem: I still had a normal life.

Daytime belonged to Java systems, maintenance work, meetings, specifications, architectural diagrams, and all the ordinary responsibilities accumulated by adulthood. Akasha had to exist in the leftover spaces.

So I started writing it in transit.

On commuter trains. In late-night diners. In quiet corners of bars. Mostly on an iPad mini.

Not as a statement about minimal computing. Quite the opposite. I simply never wanted to become the kind of person dramatically unfolding a laptop in a café as though preparing to launch a startup from table seven. I preferred disappearing into the background. With the iPad mini, people assume you are watching baseball highlights or reading the news.

Meanwhile, somewhere behind the screen, an ontology engine is being assembled.

Docker testing happened remotely through terminal software connected to rented servers. Development environments lived in browser tabs through Codespaces. The entire thing evolved under the strange constraint that it had to fit inside fragments of ordinary life.

The local-first aspect of Akasha was not originally ideological.

It was logistical.

Then one day I realized the prototype was running perfectly inside Pyto on the iPad itself.

Which was unsettling.

Because suddenly this thing I had built merely to survive my own schedule turned out to possess a property I had not consciously designed for: it could run almost anywhere.

No cloud dependency. No mandatory infrastructure. No giant machine humming somewhere in another country. Just the graph, the ontology, the local memory space, quietly existing on a small device in someone's pocket.

Stubborn. Lightweight. Self-contained.

The same qualities I've apparently been optimizing for without realizing it.

· · ·

The strangest part, though, was not that the system finally worked.

The strangest part was who it worked with.

Back in the 1990s, we thought symbolic systems and semantic networks would become the future of AI. They didn't.

That branch of the future quietly collapsed.

The people scattered.
The terminology disappeared.
The funding moved elsewhere.

What arrived instead were LLMs — statistical language systems descending from an entirely different lineage than the one we imagined.

And yet, when I began building Akasha, something deeply strange happened.

The old ideas recognized the new machine immediately.

Or perhaps the machine recognized them.

I still do not entirely understand which.

The discarded semantic structures from thirty years ago — ontologies, conceptual layers, relation models, epistemic frames — turned out to fit unusually well inside the reasoning patterns of these systems. Better, in some ways, than conventional procedural software engineering.

Hallucinations decreased.
Debugging accelerated.
Conceptual consistency emerged unexpectedly quickly.

Concept models — the part I expected to be the most fragile — became the most stable.

It felt less like inventing something new than discovering that two timelines which were never supposed to meet had somehow found a narrow bridge between them.

One future failed to arrive.

The other arrived by accident.

And somewhere in between, they shook hands.

· · ·

I built Akasha alone, at odd hours, in collaboration with an LLM — a Limited Liability Programme, you might say: useful for precise work, not responsible for the overall direction, billing me by the token.

No budget. No team. No roadmap slides.

Just the ideas from my twenties, thirty years of accumulated frustration, and a surprisingly patient thinking partner that never gets tired of being asked:

But what does this concept actually mean?

· · ·

I released it as open source. I called it Akasha — a Sanskrit word for the primordial substrate through which all things exist and connect. It seemed appropriate for something that had been waiting in a drawer for thirty years.

It runs on an iPad with no internet connection. It thinks while you sleep. It treats your private notes as dark matter — present in the database, invisible to everything outside your scope. It was built to survive in environments where nobody is maintaining it, on hardware that keeps changing, in conditions I can't predict.

And, if we are being technically accurate, it is probably at least partially composed of whisky.

· · ·

This is not a success story.

Akasha is not finished, and I'm not entirely sure what finished would even mean for a system designed to accumulate meaning over time.

The nightmare from my twenties is not resolved.

It is simply, after a very long delay, finally being worked on.

One atom at a time.
