Akasha is a local-first, concept-oriented operating system. It was built on a simple observation: humans rarely think in files. We think in meaningful chunks. A remembered sentence. A field observation. A scientific hypothesis. A fragment of a story. Most software organises these into documents and records. Akasha organises them into atoms 窶� content-addressed nodes that carry meaning independently of any container. Each atom can be linked to any other. Those links are typed 窶� they carry a relationship label, a direction, and a weight. The result is not a document. It is a semantic space that grows and evolves as you think. Traditional systems ask: how should this data be stored? Akasha asks: what kind of thing is this? Local-first. No cloud, no account, no mandatory dependencies. Runs on a laptop, a Raspberry Pi, or an iPad in the middle of an archaeological dig site. Your knowledge stays yours. DESIGN PRINCIPLES Operand ﾂｷ Operator ﾂｷ Agent Every action in Akasha is governed by one organising principle: data, operation, and subject are kept strictly separate. Operand An Atom is immutable, content-addressed data. It has no behaviour. It is not owned by any model. Operator Operations are defined independently of the data they transform. Adding a new operator is a new class file. Nothing else changes. Agent Any client 窶� a human, an LLM, a sensor, a script 窶� applies operators to atoms. The operator does not know or care which. This is deliberately different from object-oriented design, where behaviour is embedded inside data objects. In Akasha, atoms remain immutable. Concept Models 窶� structured representations of meaningful human activities like research, observation, storytelling, or analysis 窶� are operators. They act on atoms from outside. The consequence: you are the expert. The system structures what you already know. The LLM helps translate your thinking into structure and code.
